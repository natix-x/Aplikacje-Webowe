Short biography

Caravaggio (Michelangelo Merisi da Caravaggio) was born on September 29, 1571, in Milan. His father was a master builder who worked for the noble Sforza family. When Caravaggio was only six years old, his father passed away, and a few years later, he also lost his mother. After these tragic events, he moved to the nearby town of Caravaggio, from which he later took his name. As a teenager, Caravaggio began studying painting under the Milanese artist Simone Peterzano, who had been a student of Titian. His time with Peterzano exposed Caravaggio to the mannerist style and Renaissance traditions, but it was in Rome where he truly found his artistic path. Upon arriving in Rome in the 1590s, Caravaggio lived in poverty, painting small commissions such as portraits and still lifes of flowers and fruit.

However, his unique style – realistic, dramatic, and full of intense contrasts between light and shadow – soon caught the attention of Cardinal Francesco Maria del Monte, a powerful patron. With the cardinal's support, Caravaggio gained the opportunity to paint larger works, including religious commissions. His paintings shocked many because they portrayed sacred figures with an unprecedented level of honesty and naturalism. Instead of idealized images, Caravaggio painted ordinary people – rough, dirty, and weary. For example, in The Calling of Saint Matthew, Christ and Saint Matthew are depicted in a dimly lit interior, and the play of light gives the scene an almost theatrical quality. This approach garnered both admiration and outrage. Critics accused him of blasphemy, and some of his works, like Death of the Virgin, were rejected by the church.

Caravaggio was also known for his violent temper. He frequently got into fights, both on the streets and with other artists. In 1606, after one such brawl, he killed a man – reportedly over a tennis match dispute. He was sentenced to exile, and a bounty was placed on his head by the Roman authorities.

After committing murder in 1606, Caravaggio fled Rome to avoid execution. Seeking refuge, he first went to Naples and then to Malta, where he spent a significant period of his life. Malta, the stronghold of the Knights of Malta (the Sovereign Military Order of Malta), was an important cultural and religious center. Caravaggio hoped to find protection and rebuild his reputation there. On Malta, Caravaggio gained the favor of the Grand Master of the Order, Alof de Wignacourt, who was impressed by his talent. In 1608, Caravaggio was inducted as a knight of the Order of St. John, an immense honor for the artist. He painted several important works for the Order, including the monumental Beheading of Saint John the Baptist, considered one of his masterpieces. This painting is unique in that it bears Caravaggio’s only known signature, indicating how much he valued this work. However, Caravaggio’s time in Malta was also marked by turmoil. In October 1608, he got into another violent altercation, this time attacking a fellow knight. As a result, he was arrested and imprisoned. Remarkably, Caravaggio managed to escape from the Maltese fortress, which further complicated his situation and forced him to remain on the run.

After fleeing Malta, Caravaggio traveled to Sicily, where he continued to work on various commissions. Yet, he lived in constant fear of being captured. Despite the hardships and the sense of impending doom, his paintings from this period became even more dramatic and dark, reflecting his inner turmoil. The Maltese episode was both a moment of great success and a profound failure for Caravaggio, as he was unable to escape his troubles despite his artistic achievements.

Caravaggio style

Caravaggio, born Michelangelo Merisi da Caravaggio, is a pivotal figure in the history of art, renowned for his revolutionary approach to painting during the Baroque period. His unique style is characterized by several key elements:

  1. Realism: Caravaggio is celebrated for his unparalleled realism. He painted human figures with striking authenticity, eschewing idealization in favor of capturing the raw, unfiltered essence of his subjects. His characters often emerged from the streets, displaying a range of emotions and physical imperfections that rendered them relatable and human. This focus on everyday people and their experiences was a departure from the more stylized representations of earlier artistic traditions.
  2. Chiaroscuro: One of the hallmarks of Caravaggio's work is his masterful use of chiaroscuro—the technique of employing strong contrasts between light and shadow. This method creates a dramatic interplay of light that not only enhances the three-dimensionality of the figures but also evokes a sense of mood and emotion. Caravaggio's strategic placement of light draws the viewer's eye to specific elements of the composition, adding depth and intensity to the narrative.
  3. Dynamic Composition: Caravaggio's paintings often exhibit a dynamic quality, filled with movement and energy. He skillfully arranged his figures in ways that convey action and emotion, making the viewer feel as though they are part of the unfolding scene. His compositions frequently depict climactic moments, capturing the tension and drama of the narrative.
  4. Theatricality: Many of Caravaggio's works have a theatrical quality, reminiscent of staged performances. The arrangement of figures, the use of dramatic lighting, and the intense expressions all contribute to a sense of drama. This theatricality invites viewers to engage with the artwork on a deeper emotional level, as they feel drawn into the story being told.
  5. Ordinary People in Sacred Narratives: Caravaggio revolutionized religious art by populating biblical scenes with ordinary individuals. He often portrayed saints and biblical figures with relatable human traits, depicting them in everyday settings. This approach made sacred stories more accessible and resonant for viewers, bridging the gap between the divine and the mundane.
  6. Psychological Depth: Beyond the visual aspects, Caravaggio's works are rich in psychological depth. His characters often exhibit intense emotions, reflecting their inner struggles and conflicts. This emphasis on psychological realism was groundbreaking for his time and added a layer of complexity to his storytelling.

Caravaggio's innovative techniques and distinctive style had a profound influence on the development of Baroque art and the evolution of realism in painting. His legacy continues to inspire artists today, as he challenged conventions and redefined the boundaries of artistic expression. Through his dramatic narratives and compelling characters, Caravaggio remains a towering figure in the art world, embodying the power of visual storytelling.
